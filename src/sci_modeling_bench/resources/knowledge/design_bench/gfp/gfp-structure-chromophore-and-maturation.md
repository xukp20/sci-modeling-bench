# GFP Structure, Chromophore, and Maturation

## Summary

Green fluorescent protein (GFP) adopts an eleven-stranded beta-barrel
surrounding a central helix. Residues Ser65, Tyr66, and Gly67 in conventional
avGFP numbering undergo an autocatalytic intramolecular reaction that creates
the chromophore. Correct folding organizes the catalytic environment, and
molecular oxygen is required for oxidative maturation. Fluorescence depends
on both chromophore chemistry and the surrounding protein scaffold [1–4].

## Core knowledge

### Beta-barrel architecture

Crystal structures show that GFP forms a compact, eleven-stranded beta-barrel
with a distorted central alpha helix. The chromophore lies inside the barrel,
where the scaffold restricts motion, excludes much bulk solvent, and positions
residues that control maturation and photophysics [1,2].

The barrel is not merely a passive container. Packing, hydrogen bonds, and
electrostatic interactions around the chromophore affect protonation,
spectral properties, and nonradiative relaxation.

### Chromophore-forming residues

In avGFP, the backbone and side chain atoms of Ser65–Tyr66–Gly67 form the
green chromophore. Tyr66 supplies the aromatic phenolic group, while Gly67
permits the geometry required for backbone cyclization. Residue 65 influences
chromophore chemistry and protonation [2–4].

These conventional residue numbers are properties of the commonly used avGFP
reference convention. Other fluorescent proteins and processed sequences can
use different numbering.

### Autocatalytic maturation

Chromophore formation is an intramolecular process involving backbone
cyclization, dehydration, and oxidation. The folded scaffold bends and
positions the central helix so that the reacting groups approach one another.
Molecular oxygen is required for the oxidative step, while no separately
encoded enzyme is required [2–4].

Arg96 and Glu222 participate in the catalytic and electrostatic environment
of maturation. Mutations at such positions can change maturation rate or
yield even when a protein remains present [3,4].

### Protonation and excitation

Wild-type GFP contains neutral and anionic chromophore states with different
absorption bands. Excited-state proton transfer connects the chromophore to a
hydrogen-bond network involving nearby residues and water. Changes in pH or
the local interaction network can therefore alter excitation and emission
without changing protein abundance [2,4].

### Folding and fluorescence

The chromophore is encoded within the polypeptide, but productive maturation
requires the correct folded geometry. Misfolding, aggregation, incomplete
maturation, or disruption of the chromophore pocket can reduce cellular
fluorescence through distinct mechanisms.

## Conditions, limitations, and uncertainty

- Residue numbers here use conventional one-based avGFP numbering.
- Chromophore chemistry and spectra vary among GFP-like proteins and engineered
  variants.
- Correct global folding is necessary but not sufficient for identical
  fluorescence; local pocket chemistry also matters.
- Cellular fluorescence combines molecular brightness, abundance, folding,
  maturation, and measurement conditions.
