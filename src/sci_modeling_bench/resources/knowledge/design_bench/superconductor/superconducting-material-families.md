# Superconducting Material Families

## Summary

Superconductivity occurs in chemically and electronically diverse material
families. Conventional elemental and intermetallic superconductors,
magnesium diboride, cuprates, iron-based superconductors, heavy-fermion and
organic materials, and compressed hydrides do not share one simple
composition rule. Family identity provides useful context because structure,
electronic states, pairing interactions, and relevant external conditions
differ among these classes.

## Scope

### Covered

- Major superconducting material families and representative compounds.
- Broad structural and mechanistic distinctions.
- Doping and pressure conditions essential to interpreting these families.

### Not covered

- A leaderboard of compounds or transition temperatures.
- Candidate membership in a particular dataset.
- A claim that family identity uniquely determines \(T_c\).
- A complete taxonomy of every known superconductor.

## Core knowledge

### Conventional elements, alloys, and intermetallics

Many elemental metals and metallic compounds become superconducting at low
temperature. In conventional materials, electron–phonon coupling is the
principal pairing interaction described by BCS/Eliashberg theory. Relevant
material quantities include the electronic density of states, phonon
frequencies, electron–phonon coupling strength, and screened Coulomb
interaction [1,2].

A15 compounds such as \(\mathrm{Nb_3Sn}\) are intermetallic superconductors
with a characteristic \(A_3B\) crystal structure. Their superconducting and
current-carrying properties depend on phase formation, stoichiometry,
ordering, strain, and microstructure, not only on the presence of Nb or Sn
[9].

### Magnesium diboride

\(\mathrm{MgB_2}\) is a binary layered compound reported to superconduct near
39 K [3]. Its electronic structure contains distinct sigma and pi bands, and
its superconductivity is commonly described by multiband
electron–phonon-mediated pairing [10]. It is chemically and electronically
different from cuprate and iron-based high-temperature superconductors.

The example shows that a simple formula does not imply a single-band or simple
gap structure.

### Cuprates

Cuprate superconductors are layered copper oxides containing
\(\mathrm{CuO_2}\) planes. Parent compounds are commonly antiferromagnetic
charge-transfer insulators. Carrier doping—through elemental substitution,
oxygen stoichiometry, or related chemical control—can produce a
superconducting region within a broader phase diagram [4,5].

Cuprates are unconventional superconductors with a predominantly
\(d\)-wave gap. Their transition temperature depends strongly on carrier
concentration, layer structure, oxygen content, disorder, and pressure.
Merely containing Cu and O does not identify a cuprate phase or guarantee
superconductivity.

### Iron-based superconductors

Iron pnictides and chalcogenides contain conducting Fe–pnictogen or
Fe–chalcogen layers and have multiple iron-derived electronic bands near the
Fermi level. Superconductivity often occurs near magnetically ordered or
nematic phases and can be tuned by substitution, pressure, or stoichiometry
[6,7].

Major structural families include 1111, 122, 111, 11, and related layered
structures. The same elements can form nonsuperconducting phases, so a formula
must be interpreted together with structure and composition range.

### Heavy-fermion and organic superconductors

Heavy-fermion superconductors contain strongly correlated f-electron
elements, often Ce, U, or Pu, and develop very large effective electronic
masses at low temperature. Their superconductivity is frequently associated
with magnetic fluctuations rather than a simple weak-coupling phonon picture
[11].

Organic superconductors are molecular crystals in which anisotropic
electronic bands, pressure, and correlation effects are important. Both
families illustrate that elemental averages alone can miss the emergent
low-energy state [11].

### High-pressure hydrides

Hydrogen-rich compounds can have high phonon frequencies and strong
electron–phonon coupling. Several hydride phases become superconducting only
after extreme compression changes their structure and electronic state.
Sulfur hydride was reported to show conventional superconductivity near
203 K at megabar pressure, with the high-\(T_c\) phase associated with
\(\mathrm{H_3S}\)-related chemistry [8].

Pressure is part of the material state, not an optional annotation. A hydride
formula observed or predicted at megabar pressure should not be assigned the
same superconducting behavior at ambient pressure.

### Family knowledge is conditional

Chemical families provide priors about structures and mechanisms, but they
contain broad property distributions. Doping level, site occupancy, phase
purity, pressure, and preparation can move a material between superconducting
and nonsuperconducting states. Representative formulas establish scientific
examples, not deterministic rules for unmeasured compositions.

## Conditions, limitations, and uncertainty

- Family names often combine chemistry and structure; composition alone may
  not determine membership.
- “High-temperature” is used with different thresholds and historical
  contexts.
- Pairing mechanisms in unconventional families remain active research areas.
- Reported record values may require pressure or specialized sample
  preparation.
- A material can contain characteristic family elements without forming the
  required phase.
