# Elemental Properties and Periodic Trends

## Summary

Elemental properties describe isolated atoms or reference forms of elements
under specified conventions and conditions. Position in the periodic table
organizes electron configurations and produces broad trends in size,
ionization energy, electron affinity, and valence behavior. These trends are
useful scientific context, but real compounds depend on bonding, oxidation
state, structure, and collective electronic behavior; a tabulated elemental
property is not automatically the same property in a compound.

## Scope

### Covered

- Atomic mass, first ionization energy, electron affinity, atomic radius, and
  valence.
- Elemental density, heat of fusion, and thermal conductivity.
- Broad periodic trends and important definition conventions.

### Not covered

- A complete periodic-table database.
- Compound-specific oxidation states or bonding.
- A feature-selection or modeling procedure.
- Material-property values inferred from elemental averages.

## Key concepts

| Property | Physical meaning | Important convention |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Atomic mass | Mass of an atom or isotope | Isotope-specific; standard atomic weight averages stated natural sources |
| First ionization energy | Minimum energy to remove an electron from a neutral ground-state atom | Normally isolated gas-phase atom |
| Electron affinity | Energy change associated with electron attachment/removal under a stated sign convention | Sign conventions must be checked |
| Atomic radius | Effective atomic size | Covalent, metallic, ionic, and van der Waals radii differ |
| Valence | Combining capacity or electrons available under a chosen convention | Not a unique universal scalar for every element |
| Density | Mass per volume of an elemental phase | Depends on phase, temperature, pressure, and isotope composition |
| Heat of fusion | Enthalpy change on melting | Defined at a stated pressure and melting condition |
| Thermal conductivity | Heat-transport coefficient | Depends strongly on temperature, phase, purity, and microstructure |

## Core knowledge

### Atomic mass and atomic weight

An isotope has a relative atomic mass determined by its nuclear composition.
A standard atomic weight is an abundance-weighted value or interval intended
for specified normal terrestrial materials. It is not necessarily the mass of
one isotope, and elements with variable natural isotopic composition can have
interval-valued standard atomic weights [1].

Mass influences lattice dynamics through inertia, but an elemental atomic mass
alone does not determine the vibrational spectrum of a compound. Bond
stiffness, structure, and many-body interactions also matter.

### Ionization energy and electron affinity

IUPAC defines ionization energy as the minimum energy required to eject an
electron from a neutral atom or molecule in its ground state [2]. The first
ionization energy of an element is usually quoted for an isolated gas-phase
atom. Higher ionization energies remove subsequent electrons from charged
ions.

Electron affinity describes the energy associated with adding an electron to
an isolated neutral atom, or equivalently the reverse electron-detachment
process under another common convention. Tables can differ in sign. A
numerical source and convention should therefore accompany quantitative use
[3].

Across a period, increasing effective nuclear attraction often produces
smaller atomic size and higher ionization energy, but shell filling and
electron correlation create exceptions. Down a group, additional electron
shells usually increase size and reduce the binding of the outermost electron.
These are broad trends, not exact monotonic laws.

### Atomic radius is convention-dependent

An isolated atom has no sharp classical boundary. Atomic radius is inferred
from a stated physical or structural convention. Covalent radii are derived
from bond distances, metallic radii from elemental structures, ionic radii
from crystals with assigned oxidation and coordination states, and van der
Waals radii from nonbonded contacts. Different radius types should not be
silently interchanged [3].

### Valence and oxidation state

For main-group elements, group position often gives useful expectations about
valence electrons and common bonding patterns. Transition metals and
f-elements frequently exhibit several oxidation states and coordination
environments. “Valence” may denote combining capacity, a formal oxidation
state, or a count of electrons assigned to valence orbitals, depending on the
source. These quantities are related but not identical.

### Bulk properties of elemental reference phases

Density, heat of fusion, and thermal conductivity are properties of physical
samples, not isolated atoms. Their tabulated elemental values refer to a
particular phase and conditions. Allotropes can differ, thermal conductivity
can vary strongly with temperature and defects, and density changes with
thermal expansion and pressure.

When these quantities are used to describe a multielement material, the
elemental values remain reference attributes. A composition-weighted average
is a descriptor, not a mixture law guaranteed to equal the compound property.

### Periodic position carries categorical information

Period, group, and orbital block summarize electron-shell organization.
Elements in the same group often share chemical tendencies, while neighboring
transition metals can support related structures or electronic states.
However, compound behavior also depends on which orbitals participate in
bonding, formal charge, coordination, and band filling.

## Conditions, limitations, and uncertainty

- Numerical elemental tables can use different reference states, units, and
  sign conventions.
- Atomic radii and valence are families of conventions rather than uniquely
  observable scalars.
- Periodic trends contain well-known exceptions caused by shell structure and
  electron correlation.
- Properties of isolated atoms and elemental solids do not transfer linearly
  to compounds.
- Density, heat of fusion, and thermal conductivity require phase and
  temperature context.
