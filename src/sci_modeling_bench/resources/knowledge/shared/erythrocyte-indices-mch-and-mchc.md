# Erythrocyte Indices: MCH and MCHC

## Summary

Mean corpuscular hemoglobin (MCH) estimates the average mass of hemoglobin per
red blood cell. Mean corpuscular hemoglobin concentration (MCHC) estimates the
average hemoglobin concentration within the packed red-cell volume. Both are
calculated indices derived from hemoglobin and other erythrocyte measurements,
so their biological and analytical dependencies are mathematically coupled.

## Scope

This resource defines MCH and MCHC, their common formulas and units, and
general interpretation and assay limitations. It does not give a reference
range for a particular population or study.

## Core knowledge

### Definitions and formulas

With hemoglobin concentration \(\mathrm{Hb}\) in g/dL, red blood cell count
\(\mathrm{RBC}\) in millions per microliter, and hematocrit
\(\mathrm{Hct}\) as a percentage:

\[
\mathrm{MCH\ (pg/cell)}
= \frac{10 \times \mathrm{Hb\ (g/dL)}}
{\mathrm{RBC\ (10^6/\mu L)}},
\]

\[
\mathrm{MCHC\ (g/dL)}
= \frac{100 \times \mathrm{Hb\ (g/dL)}}
{\mathrm{Hct\ (\%)}}.
\]

MCH is an amount per cell. MCHC is a concentration within the volume occupied
by red cells. They are not interchangeable. Mean corpuscular volume (MCV) is
the average cell volume, and the three indices are related because hemoglobin
mass per cell, cell volume, and intracellular hemoglobin concentration are
linked.

### Dependence on measured quantities

MCH changes if measured hemoglobin changes relative to the erythrocyte count.
MCHC changes if measured hemoglobin changes relative to hematocrit. Therefore,
an error or biological shift in a component measurement propagates into the
calculated index. Two calculated indices sharing a component are statistically
correlated even without a direct causal relation between them.

Automated analyzers typically measure hemoglobin after cell lysis and obtain
RBC count and cell-volume information from intact cells. Hematocrit may be
calculated from RBC count and MCV. The exact dependency structure can therefore
vary with analyzer methodology.

### Biological interpretation

Erythrocyte indices help characterize red-cell populations. Lower hemoglobin
content or concentration can accompany impaired hemoglobin production,
iron-restricted erythropoiesis, or populations of smaller and less
hemoglobinized cells. Changes in cell volume, reticulocyte proportion, cell
hydration, and erythrocyte age distribution can shift the indices.

MCH can rise when cells are larger because a larger cell can contain more
hemoglobin while maintaining a similar intracellular concentration. MCHC is
more physically constrained than MCH because hemoglobin concentration within
an intact cell has a limited range. Interpretation should include RBC count,
hemoglobin, hematocrit, MCV, reticulocytes, and blood-smear morphology.

### Analytical artifacts

Hemolysis, lipemia, and some pigments can interfere with photometric hemoglobin
measurement. A falsely high hemoglobin result raises both calculated MCH and
MCHC. Red-cell agglutination can lower the counted RBC number and alter cell
volume, producing implausibly high calculated indices. Cell swelling or
shrinkage during storage can alter MCV and hematocrit and thereby change MCHC.

Because markedly high MCHC is unusual physiologically, it is often a signal to
check sample integrity, analyzer flags, and component measurements before
assigning a biological explanation.

### Population summaries

MCH and MCHC describe averages across circulating erythrocytes. The same mean
can arise from a uniform population or a mixture of distinct cell populations.
Distribution width and morphology provide information that a mean index does
not capture.

## Conditions, limitations, and uncertainty

Formulas depend on the stated units; changing units without the corresponding
conversion changes the numerical result. Species, strain, age, sex, analyzer,
and sample handling affect expected values. Calculated indices are not
independent observations, and isolated changes should not be interpreted
without their component measurements and the broader hematologic pattern.
