# Laboratory Rat Clinical Pathology

## Summary

Clinical pathology in laboratory rats uses hematology, serum or plasma
chemistry, coagulation, and urinalysis to characterize physiological and
pathological change. Values reflect both biology and the processes of sample
collection, handling, analysis, and reporting. Interpretation compares
individual animals and treatment groups with suitable concurrent controls and
integrates related endpoints, exposure, clinical observations, and pathology.

## Scope

This resource covers general interpretation of laboratory-rat clinical
pathology. It does not provide values for a particular study, fixed reference
ranges, or a method for predicting an endpoint.

## Core knowledge

### Main measurement domains

Hematology describes circulating cells and related variables, including red
cell mass and indices, leukocyte counts and differentials, platelets, and
reticulocytes. Clinical chemistry measures soluble analytes and enzyme
activities associated with metabolism, fluid and electrolyte balance, and
organ function. Urinalysis adds information about renal handling, hydration,
and urinary-tract changes.

No single analyte is a complete diagnosis. Related measurements form
physiological patterns: for example, a chemistry change may be interpreted
with urine findings, organ weight, and renal histopathology, while an
erythrocyte index is interpreted with hemoglobin, hematocrit, cell count,
reticulocytes, and morphology.

### Concurrent controls and reference intervals

A concurrent control group is exposed to the same laboratory conditions,
sampling schedule, handling, and vehicle as treated groups. It directly
estimates the study-specific baseline and is central to treatment comparison.

A reference interval describes the distribution expected in a defined healthy
reference population under specified conditions. It provides useful context
but is not a universal normal range. Strain, sex, age, laboratory, diet,
husbandry, analyzer, and sampling procedures can shift values. Historical
control distributions can help assess whether a group mean or individual value
is unusual, but comparability must be established.

### Biological variation

Clinical pathology values vary among animals and within the same animal over
time. Sources include genetics, sex, age, growth, feeding and fasting, hydration,
stress, circadian rhythms, activity, and subclinical disease. Group means can
hide distinct individual response patterns, while isolated extreme values can
strongly influence small groups.

Repeated measurements on the same animal are correlated. A within-animal
change from an appropriate baseline can answer a different question from a
cross-sectional comparison between independent groups.

### Preanalytical and analytical variation

Collection site, anesthesia, restraint, fasting, anticoagulant, sample volume,
clotting time, storage temperature, and delay before analysis can affect
results. Hemolysis releases intracellular constituents and can interfere with
photometric assays. Lipemia and icterus can also produce assay-dependent
interference. Small blood samples and difficult collection can cause clotting,
dilution, or cell damage.

Analyzers have method-specific calibration, precision, detection limits, and
interference. A reported value therefore combines the biological measurand
with preanalytical and analytical uncertainty.

### Interpreting a treatment-associated pattern

Evidence for a treatment relationship is strengthened by a plausible temporal
and dose pattern, consistency among animals, related analyte changes, and
agreement with clinical or pathological findings. Magnitude and biological
importance matter in addition to a statistical comparison. A statistically
significant but very small change within expected variation may not be
biologically important.

Clinical pathology can detect organ dysfunction without identifying the exact
lesion, and structural tissue injury may occur before conventional circulating
markers change. Interpretation should therefore distinguish:

- statistical difference from biological importance;
- treatment relation from adversity;
- organ function from direct tissue injury;
- a concentration change from a change in the body's total amount of an
  analyte.

## Conditions, limitations, and uncertainty

Reference values are conditional on population and method, and values from
different laboratories may not be directly comparable. Group-level summaries
do not establish that every animal responded. Sampling at one time point can
miss transient or delayed effects. Clinical pathology supports but rarely
establishes a mechanism on its own; interpretation is most reliable when
combined with exposure, longitudinal observations, and pathology.
