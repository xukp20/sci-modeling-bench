# Phosphorus Homeostasis

## Summary

Clinical chemistry commonly reports circulating inorganic phosphorus, which
exists mainly as phosphate species at physiological pH. Phosphate is essential
for bone mineral, nucleic acids, membrane phospholipids, cellular signaling,
and energy metabolism. Its extracellular concentration is governed by
intestinal absorption, exchange with bone and cells, and renal handling under
the coordinated influence of parathyroid hormone, fibroblast growth factor 23,
and vitamin D.

## Scope

This resource covers general phosphorus and phosphate physiology and the
interpretation of circulating inorganic phosphorus. It does not provide a
reference range or describe the values of a particular experiment.

## Core knowledge

### Phosphorus and phosphate terminology

Phosphorus is the chemical element. In biological fluids it is predominantly
present in oxidized phosphate forms, especially
\(\mathrm{H_2PO_4^-}\) and \(\mathrm{HPO_4^{2-}}\) near physiological pH.
Clinical laboratory labels such as phosphorus or inorganic phosphorus usually
refer to the elemental phosphorus contained in measured inorganic phosphate.

Units must be distinguished. One mmol of phosphorus contains approximately
30.97 mg of elemental phosphorus. Consequently,

\[
\mathrm{phosphorus\ (mmol/L)}
\approx 0.3229 \times \mathrm{phosphorus\ (mg/dL)}.
\]

This conversion applies when the mass result is reported as elemental
phosphorus, not as the mass of a phosphate salt.

### Distribution and biological roles

Most body phosphorus is in bone mineral, principally as hydroxyapatite with
calcium. Much of the remainder is intracellular. Only a small fraction is in
extracellular fluid, so circulating concentration does not directly measure
total-body phosphorus stores.

Phosphate participates in ATP and other energy-transfer reactions, nucleic
acids, phospholipid membranes, protein phosphorylation, intracellular
buffering, and oxygen delivery through erythrocyte 2,3-bisphosphoglycerate.

### Intestinal absorption, bone, and kidney

Dietary phosphate is absorbed from the intestine through passive and regulated
transport. Bone is both a large reservoir and a dynamic compartment that
exchanges phosphate and calcium during mineralization and resorption.

The kidneys filter phosphate and reabsorb much of it, mainly in the proximal
tubule through sodium-phosphate cotransporters. Tubular reabsorption has a
finite capacity. Renal excretion is the principal regulated route for matching
phosphate output to net input under stable conditions.

### Hormonal regulation

Parathyroid hormone (PTH) decreases renal phosphate reabsorption and thereby
promotes phosphaturia. It also participates in calcium regulation and bone
turnover.

Fibroblast growth factor 23 (FGF23), produced primarily by bone cells, lowers
renal phosphate reabsorption and reduces production of active vitamin D.
The active vitamin D metabolite, calcitriol, increases intestinal phosphate
and calcium absorption. FGF23 action depends on coreceptors including
alpha-Klotho in relevant tissues.

These regulators form feedback loops linking intestine, bone, parathyroid
glands, and kidney. A change in circulating phosphate can therefore arise from
several different mechanisms rather than one organ alone.

### Factors affecting a measured concentration

Age and growth strongly affect phosphate metabolism because skeletal
mineralization demands differ. Diet, feeding and fasting, circadian timing,
renal filtration, tubular transport, acid-base status, and endocrine signals
can change circulating phosphorus.

Phosphate is predominantly intracellular, so cell damage or hemolysis can
increase a measured sample concentration. Redistribution into or out of cells
can alter circulating phosphate without an equivalent change in total-body
stores. Reduced GFR can impair phosphate excretion, whereas increased PTH or
FGF23 can lower tubular reabsorption.

## Conditions, limitations, and uncertainty

Serum or plasma phosphorus is a concentration in a small body compartment, not
a direct measurement of skeletal content, dietary balance, or total stores.
Sampling time, feeding, age, species, renal function, and sample integrity
affect interpretation. A concentration change does not identify whether the
primary mechanism is intake, intestinal transport, cellular redistribution,
bone turnover, filtration, tubular handling, or hormonal regulation.
