# Sodium, Chloride, and Fluid and Acid-Base Homeostasis

## Summary

Sodium is the principal extracellular cation and chloride is the principal
extracellular anion. Their measured plasma or serum concentrations reflect
water distribution, renal handling, intake and loss, hormonal regulation, and
electroneutrality. Sodium concentration is primarily a relation between body
solute and water rather than a direct measure of total-body sodium. Chloride
also participates in acid-base balance through its relation to bicarbonate and
other ions.

## Scope

This resource describes general sodium and chloride physiology and
interpretation of their circulating concentrations. It does not provide
study-specific reference intervals, treatment effects, or predictive methods.

## Core knowledge

### Concentration, content, and tonicity

Most body sodium is extracellular, where sodium salts are major contributors
to effective osmolality. Plasma sodium concentration is determined by the
amounts of exchangeable sodium and potassium relative to total body water. It
can change because water changes even when total-body sodium does not change.

Thus, hyponatremia and hypernatremia are principally disorders of water
balance relative to body solute. They do not by themselves establish whether
extracellular fluid volume or total-body sodium is low, normal, or high.

Tonicity describes the osmotic effect of solutes that do not freely cross cell
membranes and therefore move water between extracellular and intracellular
compartments. Measured osmolality includes all osmotically active particles,
whereas effective osmolality excludes freely permeant solutes.

### Renal and hormonal regulation

The kidney adjusts sodium and water excretion through glomerular filtration and
segment-specific tubular reabsorption. Antidiuretic hormone, also called
vasopressin, controls water permeability in the collecting duct and is a major
regulator of water balance. Thirst changes water intake.

The renin-angiotensin-aldosterone system promotes sodium retention and supports
circulatory volume, while natriuretic peptides promote sodium excretion under
appropriate physiological conditions. Sympathetic activity, renal perfusion,
and filtration also affect sodium handling.

### Chloride and electroneutrality

Chloride commonly accompanies sodium in extracellular fluid, but the two
concentrations need not change in parallel. Every fluid compartment maintains
electroneutrality: total positive and negative charge must balance. Changes in
chloride are therefore interpreted with bicarbonate and other measured and
unmeasured ions.

Relative chloride excess commonly accompanies a lower bicarbonate
concentration in a normal-anion-gap metabolic acidosis. Relative chloride
deficit can accompany a higher bicarbonate concentration in metabolic
alkalosis. Gastrointestinal losses, renal transport, fluid administration, and
acid-base compensation can all alter chloride.

### Inputs, losses, and redistribution

Diet and administered fluids supply sodium and chloride. The kidneys provide
the main regulated route of loss, while gastrointestinal loss, sweat or other
secretions, and abnormal fluid accumulation can also matter. Water intake,
water loss, and movement between compartments can change measured
concentrations without proportional changes in total content.

Volume depletion can activate sodium-retaining systems even when circulating
sodium concentration is normal. Conversely, water retention can lower sodium
concentration while total-body sodium and extracellular volume are increased.

### Units and measurement

For monovalent ions, one mmol carries one mEq of charge, so sodium and chloride
values in mmol/L and mEq/L are numerically equal. Indirect ion-selective
electrodes measure a diluted sample and can be affected by unusually high
lipid or protein fractions; direct electrodes measure activity in undiluted
sample water and have different susceptibility to this artifact.

## Conditions, limitations, and uncertainty

A single sodium or chloride concentration does not specify volume status,
total-body stores, renal mechanism, or acid-base diagnosis. Interpretation
requires the clinical context and often osmolality, bicarbonate, potassium,
urea, glucose, urine measurements, intake, losses, and administered fluids.
Species, age, diet, collection method, and analyzer affect expected laboratory
values. Preanalytical dilution or contamination can produce spurious results.
