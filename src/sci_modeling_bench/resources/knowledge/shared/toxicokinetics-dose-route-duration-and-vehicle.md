# Toxicokinetics: Dose, Route, Duration, and Vehicle

## Summary

Toxicokinetics relates administered dose to the concentration and time course
of a substance and its metabolites in the body. Absorption, distribution,
metabolism, and excretion determine internal exposure. Route, formulation,
vehicle, dosing interval, duration, species, sex, and dose-dependent processes
can make equal nominal doses produce different systemic or tissue exposures.

## Scope

This resource covers general toxicokinetic concepts used to interpret
toxicology experiments. It does not provide pharmacokinetic parameters for any
specific compound or recommend how to rank compounds.

## Core knowledge

### Administered dose and internal exposure

An administered dose is meaningful only with a quantity, unit, route, and time
basis. In animal studies it is often normalized by body mass, but other
expressions are possible. The administered amount is not the same as the amount
that reaches systemic circulation or a target tissue.

Bioavailability is the fraction of an administered dose reaching systemic
circulation in unchanged form. For an extravascular route, absolute
bioavailability can be expressed as

\[
F =
\frac{\mathrm{AUC}_{\mathrm{route}}/\mathrm{Dose}_{\mathrm{route}}}
{\mathrm{AUC}_{\mathrm{IV}}/\mathrm{Dose}_{\mathrm{IV}}},
\]

where AUC is the area under the concentration-time curve and intravenous
administration is the reference. Tissue exposure can still differ from plasma
exposure because of distribution, binding, transport, and local metabolism.

### Concentration-time measures

Common exposure measures include:

- \(C_{\max}\), the observed maximum concentration;
- \(t_{\max}\), the time at which \(C_{\max}\) occurs;
- AUC, the integral of concentration over time;
- terminal half-life, the time associated with the terminal decline;
- clearance, the proportionality between elimination rate and concentration;
- volume of distribution, an apparent volume relating amount in the body to
  measured concentration.

Different toxic responses can relate to different exposure features. A rapid
effect may track peak concentration, while cumulative injury may track AUC,
duration above a threshold, or tissue burden.

### Route and formulation

Oral exposure is influenced by dissolution, gastrointestinal stability,
absorption, and first-pass metabolism. Intravenous administration bypasses
absorption and gives complete entry into the systemic circulation, while other
routes have their own absorption barriers and local tissue exposure.

The vehicle and formulation affect solubility, stability, release rate,
absorption, local irritation, and sometimes physiology. Vehicle-treated
controls help identify changes caused by the administration procedure or
vehicle itself. Results from different routes or formulations are not directly
interchangeable solely because the nominal dose is the same.

### Repeated dosing and accumulation

With repeated administration, concentrations depend on the dosing interval
relative to elimination. If a new dose is given before the previous dose is
fully eliminated, the substance or a metabolite can accumulate. Under
time-invariant linear kinetics, repeated dosing approaches a periodic steady
state after several effective half-lives. Enzyme induction, inhibition,
physiological adaptation, or progressive organ injury can make kinetics change
over time.

### Dose proportionality and nonlinear kinetics

For linear kinetics, AUC is proportional to dose and clearance is constant.
At higher or lower doses, saturation of absorption, protein binding,
metabolism, transport, or excretion can break this proportionality. A nominal
dose increase can then cause a smaller or larger proportional change in
exposure.

Toxicokinetic measurements across dose groups help determine whether the
animals were exposed and whether exposure increased with dose. They also help
distinguish a weak biological response caused by low internal exposure from
one caused by low tissue sensitivity.

### Biological sources of variation

Species, strain, sex, age, body composition, health, feeding state, and
circadian timing can affect pharmacokinetics. Metabolites can have activity or
toxicity distinct from the parent compound. Plasma concentration is often a
useful exposure measure but may not represent the relevant concentration at a
specific target organ.

## Conditions, limitations, and uncertainty

Sparse sampling can miss \(C_{\max}\) or poorly estimate AUC and half-life.
Assay measurements may combine active and inactive chemical forms. Nominal
dose without a unit, route, schedule, and formulation is incomplete exposure
information. Association between plasma exposure and toxicity does not by
itself establish mechanism, and similar plasma AUC values can conceal
different peaks, tissue distributions, or metabolite profiles.
